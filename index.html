Understanding Sharia Law

What is Sharia Law?

Sharia is Islam's legal framework for moral guidance and legal principles derived covering all aspects of life:

  • Worship (Ibadah): Prayer, fasting, zakat, pilgrimage
  • Personal Life (Muamalat): Food, dress, marriage, ethics
  • Social/Commerce: Business, inheritance, contracts, crimes

Sharia is interpreted by scholars in context (faqih, mujtahid) to adapt to varying circumstances, guided by divine revelation:

وَأَنِ احْكُم بَيْنَهُم بِمَا أَنزَلَ اللَّهُ وَلَا تَتَّبِعْ أَهْوَاءَهُمْ (Quran 5:49) "And judge between them by what Allah has revealed and do not follow their inclinations"
من يرد الله به خيرا يفقهه في الدين (Sahih Bukhari 71 & Muslim 1037) "Whomever Allah wishes good for, He gives understanding of the religion"

Core Characteristics of Sharia

  • Divine Origin: Based on revelation (Quran) and Prophetic example (Sunnah)
  • Comprehensive: Covers all aspects of human life and behavior
  • Purposive: Aimed at achieving human welfare (maslaha)
  • Flexible: Contains mechanisms for adaptation to changing circumstances
  • Ethical: Emphasizes moral obligations alongside legal duties

Maqasid al-Sharia (Higher Objectives)

1

Protection of Faith

Freedom of religious practice and preservation of religious values, including:

  • Protection of places of worship
  • Religious education and guidance
  • Respecting sanctity of religious symbols
2

Protection of Life

Detailed rules on justice in capital cases and sanctity of life, including:

  • Strong burden of proof in criminal cases
  • Healthcare provisions and medical ethics
  • Prohibition of suicide and euthanasia
3

Protection of Intellect

Preserving mental capacities and encouraging intellectual growth:

  • Prohibition of intoxicants
  • Pursuit of knowledge as religious duty
  • Freedom of thought within ethical boundaries
4

Protection of Lineage

Preserving family structure and clear lineage relations:

  • Marriage as legal framework for family
  • Rights of children to know their parentage
  • Prohibition of adultery and false paternity claims
5

Protection of Property

Safeguarding wealth and economic rights:

  • Prohibition of theft, fraud, and usury
  • Rules for fair contracts and transactions
  • Rights of ownership and inheritance laws
  • Economic redistribution through zakat (charity)

Primary & Secondary Sources

Quran

  • Exact words of God
  • 114 surahs in classical Arabic
  • Legal verses (ayat) comprise 14% content

Sunnah

  • Authentic hadeeths compiled in collections
  • Clarifies and details Qur'anic injunctions
  • Includes actions, approvals, and inactions of the Prophet

Ijma (Scholarly) Consensus

Applications in:

  • Contemporary medical ethics (bioethics, etc.)
  • Financial transactions (Islamic banking rulings)
  • New technological challenges

Analogical Reasoning

  • Applies existing laws to new circumstances
  • Derivation of similar causes (illah)
  • Used in modern jurisprudence (fiqh muasar)

Madhhabs (Schools of Thought)

Hanafi School

Founded by Imam Abu Hanifa (d.767)

  • Most widely followed school globally
  • Emphasizes analogical reasoning (qiyas)
  • Prevalent in Turkey, Balkans, Central Asia, South Asia
  • Known for flexibility and pragmatism

Maliki School

Founded by Imam Malik ibn Anas (d.795)

  • Dominant in North Africa
  • Primacy given to Medinan practices
  • Strong emphasis on public interest (maslaha)
  • Employs (block evas) to prevent harm

Shafi'i School

Founded by Imam al-Shafi'i (d.820)

  • Prevalent in Egypt, Horn of Africa, Southeast Asia
  • Systematized usul al-fiqh (principles of jurisprudence)
  • Balances textual evidence with reasoning
  • Created formal methodology for deriving rulings

Hanbali School

Founded by Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal (d.855)

  • Predominant in Saudi Arabia and Qatar
  • Most literal interpretation of texts
  • Minimizes role of personal opinion in rulings
  • Strict adherence to authentic hadith

Ja'fari School

Named after Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq (d.765)

  • Main Shia jurisprudential school
  • Prevalent in Iran, Iraq, and parts of Lebanon
  • Emphasizes role of Imams in interpretation
  • Distinct approach to inheritance and marriage laws

Common Misconceptions

Sharia covers all aspects of life including charity, prayer, marriage, business ethics, inheritance and more. Criminal law is a small portion.

Many Islamic scholars argue that core Sharia principles align with democratic values like justice, consultation and protection of rights.

While core principles derived from the Quran and Sunnah are considered divine and unchangeable, much of Sharia is interpretative and adaptable to changing times and circumstances through ijtihad (scholarly reasoning).

Classical Sharia granted women rights to education, property ownership, inheritance, and divorce centuries before Western legal systems. Modern interpretations vary widely based on cultural context rather than religious text.

No, Sharia varies significantly across regions and countries due to different schools of thought, cultural influences, and the extent to which it's incorporated into civil legal systems.

Contemporary Fatawa (Legal Rulings)

Modern scholars have issued numerous fatawa on contemporary issues, adapting classical principles to new contexts:

Cryptocurrency (IslamQA.org)

Many scholars consider cryptocurrencies permissible if they:

  • Have real utility and value
  • Are not primarily speculative instruments
  • Do not involve excessive uncertainty (gharar)
  • Are not used for illicit purposes

Medical Necessity (SeekersGuidance)

The principle of necessity (darura) permits normally prohibited actions when necessary for preserving life:

  • Use of prohibited medications containing alcohol
  • Organ transplantation under specific conditions
  • Medical procedures during Ramadan fasting
  • Gender-specific treatment accommodations

Digital Privacy (Harvard ShariaSources)

Modern fatawa have extended privacy protection to digital realms based on:

  • The Quranic prohibition of spying
  • The sanctity of private information
  • Protection of personal dignity (karama)
  • Consent requirements for data sharing

Qawa'id Fiqhiyyah (Legal Maxims)

According to Harvard SHARIAsource and SeekersGuidance, Islamic legal theory employs overarching maxims to derive rulings:

الأمور بمقاصدها
Al-umūru bi-maqāṣidihā
"Acts are judged by intentions"

The validity of actions depends on the intentions behind them. This maxim is derived from the famous hadith:

"Indeed, actions are based on intentions, and each person will be rewarded according to their intention."

Applications in modern contexts include:

  • Contract law: Determines validity based on parties' intentions
  • Criminal law: Distinguishes between intentional and unintentional acts
  • Financial transactions: Rules on permissibility based on purpose
اليقين لا يزول بالشك
Al-yaqīnu lā yazūlu bi-l-shakk
"Certainty is not removed by doubt"

An established fact remains valid until definitive evidence proves otherwise. From IslamQA sources:

  • Ritual purity (tahara) remains valid until definitely broken
  • Property ownership persists until transfer is verified
  • Contracts remain valid until proven otherwise
  • Used in Islamic evidentiary standards and burden of proof
المشقة تجلب التيسير
Al-mashaqqa tajlibu al-taysīr
"Hardship begets facility"

According to OCU Islamic Law Guide, when application of a rule causes undue hardship, concessions are permitted. Examples include:

  • Prayer shortening during travel
  • Fasting exemptions for illness or travel
  • Modern applications in medical treatment during Ramadan
  • Economic relief provisions during financial hardship
الضرر يزال
Al-ḍararu yuzāl
"Harm must be eliminated"

From Islamic-Sharia.org, this principle establishes that harm prevention is a foundational goal:

  • Environmental protection requirements
  • Consumer protection regulations
  • Medical ethics decisions
  • Grounds for divorce in cases of abuse

Modern applications include prohibition of harmful industries and activities that damage society.

العادة محكمة
Al-'ādatu muḥakkamah
"Custom is authoritative"

According to Harvard's Islamic Legal Studies, local customs ('urf) are considered a valid source of law when not contradicting explicit Islamic text:

  • Commercial practices specific to different regions
  • Marriage customs that vary by culture
  • Standards for maintenance and financial support
  • Forms of contracts and business dealings

This principle allows Sharia's adaptability to different societies and eras.

Quranic Foundations of Sharia

The Quran provides the primary foundation for Islamic Sharia Law. Here are key verses that establish core legal principles:

وَأَنزَلْنَا إِلَيْكَ الْكِتَابَ بِالْحَقِّ مُصَدِّقًا لِّمَا بَيْنَ يَدَيْهِ مِنَ الْكِتَابِ وَمُهَيْمِنًا عَلَيْهِ فَاحْكُم بَيْنَهُم بِمَا أَنزَلَ اللَّهُ
Surat Al-Ma'idah 5:48
"And We have revealed to you the Book in truth, confirming that which preceded it of the Scripture and as a criterion over it. So judge between them by what Allah has revealed."

Legal Significance:

This establishes divine revelation as the primary source of law and commandment to judge by God's revelation. It forms the basis for deriving law from the Quran.

يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا أَطِيعُوا اللَّهَ وَأَطِيعُوا الرَّسُولَ وَأُولِي الْأَمْرِ مِنكُمْ فَإِن تَنَازَعْتُمْ فِي شَيْءٍ فَرُدُّوهُ إِلَى اللَّهِ وَالرَّسُولِ
Surat An-Nisa 4:59
"O you who believe! Obey Allah and obey the Messenger and those in authority among you. And if you disagree over anything, refer it to Allah and the Messenger."

Legal Significance:

This verse establishes the second source of Islamic law (the Sunnah) and the hierarchical process for resolving disputes by referring to divine sources.

لَا إِكْرَاهَ فِي الدِّينِ قَد تَّبَيَّنَ الرُّشْدُ مِنَ الْغَيِّ
Surat Al-Baqarah 2:256
"There shall be no compulsion in religion. The right course has become clear from the wrong."

Legal Significance:

This establishes freedom of religious choice and informs the Sharia principle of respecting non-Muslims' religious autonomy in Islamic states.

يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا كُونُوا قَوَّامِينَ بِالْقِسْطِ شُهَدَاءَ لِلَّهِ وَلَوْ عَلَىٰ أَنفُسِكُمْ
Surat An-Nisa 4:135
"O you who have believed, be persistently standing firm in justice, witnesses for Allah, even if it be against yourselves."

Legal Significance:

This establishes fair testimony as a cornerstone of Sharia judicial proceedings, requiring justice even when it goes against self-interest or family ties.

وَأَوْفُوا بِالْعَهْدِ إِنَّ الْعَهْدَ كَانَ مَسْئُولًا
Surat Al-Isra 17:34
"And fulfill [every] commitment. Indeed, the commitment is ever [that about which one will be] questioned."

Legal Significance:

This verse forms the basis for contract law in Sharia, emphasizing the binding nature of agreements and commitments in commercial and civil transactions.

وَلَكُمْ فِي الْقِصَاصِ حَيَاةٌ يَا أُولِي الْأَلْبَابِ لَعَلَّكُمْ تَتَّقُونَ
Surat Al-Baqarah 2:179
"And there is for you in legal retribution [saving of] life, O you [people] of understanding, that you may become righteous."

Legal Significance:

This establishes the principle of proportional justice (qisas) in criminal law, explaining that fair punishment serves as both a deterrent and a protection for society.

Hadith Foundations of Islamic Law

The Sunnah (Prophet Muhammad's sayings and actions) forms the second primary source of Islamic law. Here are key Hadith that establish fundamental legal principles:

إِنَّمَا الْأَعْمَالُ بِالنِّيَّاتِ وَإِنَّمَا لِكُلِّ امْرِئٍ مَا نَوَى، فَمَنْ كَانَتْ هِجْرَتُهُ إِلَى اللَّهِ وَرَسُولِهِ فَهِجْرَتُهُ إِلَى اللَّهِ وَرَسُولِهِ، وَمَنْ كَانَتْ هِجْرَتُهُ لِدُنْيَا يُصِيبُهَا أَوْ امْرَأَةٍ يَنْكِحُهَا فَهِجْرَتُهُ إِلَى مَا هَاجَرَ إِلَيْهِ
"Actions are judged by intentions, and each person will have what they intended. So whoever emigrates for Allah and His Messenger, then his emigration is for Allah and His Messenger. And whoever emigrates to get something in the world or to marry a woman, his emigration is for whatever he emigrated for."
Sahih al-Bukhari 1 and Sahih Muslim 1907

Legal Principle:

This hadith establishes the fundamental maxim of Islamic jurisprudence that intentions determine the validity of actions and their legal consequences.

Applications:

  • Determination of criminality in criminal matters
  • Validity of contracts and business dealings
  • Classification of religious acts of worship
لاَ ضَرَرَ وَلاَ ضِرَارَ
"There should be neither harm nor reciprocating harm."
Sunan Ibn Majah 2340

Legal Principle:

This establishes the prohibition of causing harm or reciprocating harm, forming one of the most important legal maxims in Islamic jurisprudence.

Applications:

  • Environmental protection regulations
  • Neighbor and property rights
  • Medical ethics and treatments
  • Business and consumer protection laws
الْبَيِّنَةُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعِي وَالْيَمِينُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعَى عَلَيْهِ
"The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the one who denies."
Sunan al-Tirmidhi 1341

Legal Principle:

This hadith establishes fundamental principles of Islamic evidence law and procedural justice.

Applications:

  • Civil dispute resolution
  • Criminal trial procedures
  • Commercial dispute resolution
  • The presumption of innocence until proven guilty
كُلُّ مُسْكِرٍ خَمْرٌ، وَكُلُّ خَمْرٍ حَرَامٌ
"Every intoxicant is khamr (wine), and every khamr is forbidden."
Sahih Muslim 2003

Legal Principle:

This hadith demonstrates analogical reasoning (qiyas) by extending the prohibition to all intoxicants, not just those known at the time.

Applications:

  • Prohibition of modern drugs and substances
  • Regulation of medications containing alcohol
  • Food and beverage standards
  • Medical exceptions under necessity
الصُّلْحُ جَائِزٌ بَيْنَ الْمُسْلِمِينَ إِلَّا صُلْحًا حَرَّمَ حَلَالًا أَوْ أَحَلَّ حَرَامًا
"Reconciliation is permissible among Muslims, except for a reconciliation that makes the lawful unlawful, or the unlawful lawful."
Sunan Abi Dawud 3594

Legal Principle:

This establishes the freedom of contract principle and the permissibility of arbitration and settlements, within Sharia boundaries.

Applications:

  • Alternative dispute resolution
  • Negotiated settlements in civil cases
  • Business contracts and agreements
  • Limits on what can be validly contracted
